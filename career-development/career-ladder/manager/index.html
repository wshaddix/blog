# Manager of Software Engineering

As a Manager, you shift from individual technical contribution to team enablement. Your primary focus is on team execution and people development. You answer the question: "How do we deliver this?"

# Core Responsibilities

# People Management

  • Conduct regular 1:1s with each team member
  • Provide feedback, coaching, and career development guidance
  • Write performance reviews and manage compensation discussions
  • Handle performance issues and difficult conversations
  • Build an inclusive, psychologically safe team environment

# Team Delivery

  • Ensure the team delivers on sprint/quarterly commitments
  • Remove blockers and shield the team from distractions
  • Manage team capacity and workload distribution
  • Balance technical debt with feature development

# Hiring & Team Building

  • Screen resumes and conduct interviews
  • Make hiring decisions for the team
  • Onboard new team members effectively
  • Build team culture and improve team dynamics

# Process & Operations

  • Implement and refine agile processes (standups, planning, retros)
  • Establish code review and quality standards
  • Manage on-call rotations and incident response
  • Track and report on team metrics

# Stakeholder Management

  • Partner with product manager on roadmap and priorities
  • Coordinate with design, QA, and adjacent teams
  • Communicate team status to leadership
  • Represent the team in cross-functional discussions

# Key Skill Sets

Category Skills
Technical Strong enough to review code, participate in design, make architectural decisions for the team; can debug and unblock engineers
People Leadership Coaching, giving feedback, conflict resolution, career development, building trust
Communication Running effective meetings, stakeholder updates, written documentation, active listening
Project Management Sprint planning, backlog grooming, estimation, delivery tracking, risk management
Hiring Interviewing, evaluating candidates, selling the team/company, making hiring decisions
Organizational Understanding team dynamics, navigating company politics, managing up

# Typical Day-to-Day Activities

Morning

  • Review team dashboards: PRs pending, CI status, blockers
  • Triage Slack/email for urgent issues
  • Prepare for the day's meetings and 1:1s

Recurring Meetings

  • Daily standup with the team
  • 1:1s with direct reports (5-10 hrs/week total)
  • Sprint planning, backlog grooming, retrospectives
  • Syncs with product manager and design lead
  • Team lead or manager sync with peers

Technical Work

  • Review pull requests (3-8 hrs/week)
  • Participate in design discussions
  • Debug critical issues or help unblock engineers
  • Interview candidates (2-4 interviews/week)

People Management

  • Career conversations and feedback sessions
  • Performance review preparation
  • Addressing team conflicts or concerns
  • Onboarding support for new hires

Administrative

  • Update project status and roadmap progress
  • Prepare updates for leadership
  • Process approvals (time off, expenses, etc.)

# Comparison: Manager vs Director

Dimension Manager Director
Team size 5-12 engineers (single team) 15-50+ engineers (multiple teams)
Direct reports Individual contributors Managers and/or senior ICs
Span One squad or product area Multiple squads, a domain, or product line
Reports to Director or Senior Director VP or Senior Director
Planning horizon Sprint to quarterly Quarterly to annual
Budget authority Typically none May own headcount and tooling budget
Hiring Screens and interviews for their team Defines hiring strategy, approves offers across teams
Technical involvement Deep—reviews PRs, participates in design Broader—sets standards, approves architecture
Performance mgmt Writes reviews, manages PIPs, promotes ICs Calibrates across teams, promotes managers
Process ownership Implements team processes Defines org-wide engineering processes
Stakeholder mgmt Product manager, design lead, adjacent teams Multiple PMs, senior leadership, cross-functional execs
Incident response Leads team response, on-call escalation Oversees response, handles escalations to leadership

# Key Differences Summary

Dimension Manager Director
Metaphor Team captain on the field Coach managing multiple teams
Success metric Team velocity, quality, retention Org-wide delivery, manager effectiveness, strategic impact
Failure mode Team misses deadlines, attrition Cross-team misalignment, poor manager development, unclear strategy

# Transition to Director

# Timeline

Typically 3-5 years as a manager, though varies by company size and growth rate.

# Key Shifts Required

From (Manager) To (Director)
Owning a team Owning a domain
Solving problems Defining which problems to solve
Executing the roadmap Shaping the roadmap
Managing ICs Managing managers
Team metrics Org-wide metrics
Tactical decisions Strategic decisions

# Key Actions to Make the Transition

1. Build Leadership Bench

  • Identify and develop future leads/managers on your team
  • Delegate increasingly complex responsibilities
  • Create a team that can operate without you in the room

Example: Identify a senior engineer with leadership potential. Give them ownership of a project, let them run standups, and coach them through people challenges. Advocate for their promotion to tech lead.

2. Expand Your Scope Intentionally

  • Volunteer for cross-team initiatives
  • Lead projects that span multiple squads
  • Own a company-wide technical or process initiative

Example: Propose and lead a company-wide initiative to improve CI/CD pipeline performance. Coordinate with 3 teams, present progress to leadership, and deliver measurable improvements.

3. Develop Strategic Thinking

  • Propose roadmap items, not just execute them
  • Write strategy docs connecting team work to business outcomes
  • Present quarterly plans to leadership

Example: Write a proposal for a new technical investment, including business case, resource requirements, and expected outcomes. Present it to your Director and VP for approval.

4. Build Cross-Functional Relationships

  • Partner directly with senior PMs, Design leads, and other Directors
  • Become a trusted advisor outside your immediate team
  • Understand the business deeply (revenue, customers, market)

Example: Schedule regular coffee chats with Directors from other departments. Learn about their challenges and find ways to collaborate. Become the go-to engineering contact for product leadership.

5. Get Exposure to "Director-Level" Problems

  • Ask your Director to include you in org planning
  • Participate in headcount and budget discussions
  • Handle escalations that would normally go to your Director

Example: Ask to attend quarterly planning sessions. Volunteer to present your team's roadmap. Take notes on how Directors discuss tradeoffs and priorities.

6. Demonstrate Manager-of-Managers Skills

  • Mentor tech leads or new managers informally
  • Lead calibration discussions for your team
  • Show you can coach others on people management

Example: When a peer becomes a new manager, offer to be their mentor. Meet weekly to help them navigate challenges like difficult 1:1s, performance issues, and team dynamics.

# Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Staying too hands-on technically (can't let go)
  • Only focusing on your team's success vs. org success
  • Not building relationships with senior leadership
  • Waiting to be promoted instead of operating at the next level

# Career Progression Indicators

You're ready for Director when you've demonstrated:

  • Successfully scaled a team or built a new team from scratch
  • Ability to mentor other leads or managers
  • Delivered cross-team or cross-functional initiatives
  • Strategic thinking beyond immediate team scope
  • Strong relationships with senior stakeholders
  • Handled organizational challenges (reorgs, attrition, performance issues)
