# Director of Software Engineering

As a Director, you shift from managing a single team to owning a domain across multiple teams. Your primary focus is on cross-team strategy and organizational health. You answer the question: "What should we build and why?"

# Core Responsibilities

# Strategic Leadership

  • Define and execute the technical vision aligned with business objectives
  • Own the engineering roadmap and prioritization across multiple teams
  • Drive architectural decisions and technical standards
  • Manage engineering budgets and resource allocation

# People Management

  • Lead and mentor engineering managers and senior ICs
  • Build, scale, and retain high-performing engineering teams
  • Conduct performance reviews, career development, and succession planning
  • Foster an inclusive, collaborative engineering culture

# Execution & Delivery

  • Ensure on-time, high-quality delivery of products and features
  • Establish and track engineering KPIs (velocity, quality, reliability)
  • Manage cross-functional dependencies and remove blockers
  • Balance technical debt reduction with feature development

# Organizational

  • Partner with Product, Design, and other stakeholders on strategy
  • Communicate engineering status and risks to executive leadership
  • Represent engineering in company-wide planning and decisions
  • Drive process improvements and operational excellence

# Typical Day-to-Day Activities

Morning

  • Review dashboards: team velocity, incidents, deployment metrics
  • Triage urgent escalations or blockers from managers
  • Async catch-up on Slack/email from cross-functional partners

Recurring Meetings

  • 1:1s with engineering managers (4-6 hrs/week)
  • Staff/leadership sync with direct reports
  • Cross-functional syncs (Product, Design, QA, Platform)
  • Architecture or technical design reviews
  • Sprint demos or milestone reviews

Strategic Work

  • Roadmap planning and prioritization sessions
  • Headcount planning and hiring pipeline reviews
  • Interview debriefs for senior candidates
  • Writing technical strategy docs or RFCs
  • Performance calibration and career conversations

Ad-hoc

  • Incident response and post-mortems
  • Unblocking teams on technical or organizational issues
  • Mentoring high-potential ICs or managers
  • Vendor evaluations or build-vs-buy decisions

# Typical Scope

  • Direct reports: 4-8 engineering managers
  • Org size: 30-100+ engineers
  • Teams: Multiple squads or domains

# Key Differences Summary

Dimension Director VP
Metaphor Coach managing multiple teams General manager running the franchise
Success metric Org-wide delivery, manager effectiveness, strategic impact Business outcomes, org health, talent retention at scale
Failure mode Cross-team misalignment, poor manager development, unclear strategy Missed business targets, executive credibility loss, org dysfunction

Key distinction: A Director is a "player-coach" who remains closer to execution and technical decisions. A VP is primarily an executive leader focused on organizational strategy, culture, and business outcomes.

# Key Actions to Make the Transition

1. Think Like an Executive

  • Understand the full P&L, not just engineering costs
  • Frame engineering decisions in terms of business impact
  • Develop opinions on company strategy, not just engineering strategy

Example: Before proposing a major infrastructure investment, build a business case showing ROI: "This $500K investment in platform reliability will reduce customer churn by X%, saving $2M annually."

2. Build Executive Relationships

  • Partner directly with VP/C-level peers in Product, Sales, Finance
  • Earn trust of the CEO/CTO as a strategic thought partner
  • Navigate board-level discussions and investor expectations

Example: Schedule monthly lunch meetings with the VP of Product and VP of Sales. Understand their challenges and find ways engineering can help. Become a trusted partner, not just a service provider.

3. Own Organizational Design

  • Lead a reorg or team restructure
  • Define career ladders and leveling frameworks
  • Shape engineering culture at scale

Example: Propose and lead a reorganization to better align teams with product areas. Define the new structure, manage the transition, communicate changes, and measure the impact on delivery and retention.

4. Develop External Presence

  • Speak at conferences, write blog posts
  • Become a talent magnet for senior hires
  • Represent the company in technical partnerships

Example: Submit a talk proposal to a major conference about a technical challenge your team solved. Write blog posts about engineering culture. Become known in the industry as a thought leader.

5. Handle Ambiguity and Crisis

  • Lead through major incidents, layoffs, or pivots
  • Make high-stakes decisions with incomplete information
  • Maintain team morale during uncertainty

Example: When a major production incident occurs, take charge of the executive response. Communicate with the board, manage customer communications, and lead the post-mortem that results in systemic improvements.

6. Demonstrate Scalable Leadership

  • Build a strong Director bench that operates autonomously
  • Create systems and processes that scale beyond your direct involvement
  • Show you can grow the org 2-3x without proportional effort increase

Example: Develop your Directors so well that they can run their domains without your daily involvement. Create processes for hiring, onboarding, and performance management that scale.

7. Get P&L or Business Ownership

  • Own a product line end-to-end (engineering, budget, outcomes)
  • Drive build-vs-buy decisions with financial accountability
  • Partner on M&A technical due diligence

Example: Take ownership of a product P&L, not just the engineering budget. Be accountable for revenue and cost targets. Present quarterly results to the executive team.

# Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Staying too focused on engineering vs. business outcomes
  • Not building a strong enough leadership bench
  • Avoiding organizational politics (you must navigate them)
  • Lack of executive communication skills
  • Not developing external presence and network

# Career Progression Indicators

You're ready for VP when you've demonstrated:

  • Ability to set and execute multi-year technical strategy
  • Strong relationships with C-level executives
  • Track record of building and developing Directors
  • Experience navigating organizational change at scale
  • External visibility and thought leadership
  • Business acumen beyond engineering metrics
  • Composure and judgment during crises
